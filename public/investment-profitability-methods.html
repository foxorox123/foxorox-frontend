Investment Profitability Methods

4.1 Payback Period Method

The payback period method is the simplest approach to estimating investment profitability. It determines the time required for net cash inflows to cover the initial investment expenditure. A project is considered acceptable when the payback period is shorter than a defined critical value.

Investment risk, measured as a function \( E[X] \) and variance \( V[X] \), where \( E \) is the expected value and \( X \) is the analyzed variable, is determined by the interest rate \( i \) which justifies the payback period. It is assumed that all investment risk is included in the interest rate—i.e., all external risk factors are reflected in \( i \).

In practice, countries with high investment risk—whether political or macroeconomic—tend to maintain elevated interest rates to prevent capital outflows. In such cases, central banks or governments often increase the interest rate \( I \). This move is usually preceded by depreciation of the local currency or a drop in government bond prices (rise in yields).

Let the minimal return rate from investment \( r_i \) in period \( i \) be equal to the interest rate \( I_i \). Then: 

E(r)=∑_(i=1)^n h_i r_i

where:

  • E(r) – expected return rate,
  • h_i – probability of occurrence of a particular rate of return,
  • r_i – rate of return in period i.

Assuming \( I_i=r_i \), the probabilities h_i can be modeled through economic growth factors using the Cobb–Douglas production function: 

F=f(A,K,L,R)

where:

  • A – technological level in the economy,
  • K – productive capital input,
  • L – labor input,
  • R – natural resources input.

Usually, the Cobb–Douglas function is presented with two variables: 

F(K,L)=aK^α L^β,  K,L ≥ 0

where α and β are scaling parameters and α+β=1 implies constant returns to scale. When α+β>1, there are increasing returns to scale; when α+β<1, there are decreasing returns to scale.

In general form:

F(X₁,X₂,…,X_N)=a∏_(i=1)^N X_i^(α_i),  X_i≥0

The function is non-negative and increasing. When ∑α_i=1, the function is homogeneous of degree one, i.e.: 

∀z>0: F(zX₁,zX₂,…,zX_N)=zF(X₁,X₂,…,X_N)

Hence, constant returns to scale apply.

Probabilistic Space

Let (Ω,F,P) be a probability space where F is a σ-algebra on Ω and elements of F are pairs (K_i,L_i) defining capital and labor inputs for each period i. 


P(K_i,L_i) ≥ 0 for all events K_i,L_i∈F
P(Ω)=1 for Ω∈F
For a countable family of disjoint sets:
P(⋃_(i∈I)(K_i,L_i))=∑_(i∈I)P(K_i,L_i)
with (K_i,L_i)∩(K_j,L_j)=∅ for i≠j

This defines a valid probability measure as a function of (K,L), allowing productivity growth modeling using Cobb–Douglas.

Assign:

P(K_i,L_i)=h_i

Then:

E(r)=∑_(i=1)^n P(K_i,L_i)r_i

From such a probabilistic model, we can forecast expected return rates relative to economic growth.

Macroeconomic States

For simplicity, probabilities can correspond to macroeconomic states defined by interest rate levels:

  • Hyperinflation – I > 1000% per year
  • Controlled inflation – I ≤ 100% per year
  • Low inflation – I ≤ 10% per year
  • Stable inflation – I ≤ 2% per year
  • No inflation – I ≈ 0%
  • Deflation – I between -0.5% and -0.1%
  • High deflation – I ≈ -1%
  • Hyperdeflation – I ≈ -10%

States 7 and 8 are theoretical, rarely seen in modern economies, while hyperinflation (state 1) occurred historically many times.

4.2 Accounting Rate of Return (ARR)

This method uses accounting data to assess a company’s performance. ARR measures the relationship between average net income (NI) and the average book value (BV) of the investment project. A project is acceptable if ARR exceeds the company’s minimum acceptable rate. 


NI=∑_(i=1)^N NI_i/(N−1)
BV=∑_(i=1)^N BV_i/(N−1)
r_ks=NI/BV=(∑_(i=1)^N NI_i/(N−1))/(∑_(i=1)^N BV_i/(N−1))

Book value BV_i depends on income in the next period: 

BV_(i+1)=f(NI_i)

The function f(NI_i) determines income growth. Hence: 


BV_(i+1)=f(E(NI))
E(NI)=∑_(i=1)^n P(K_i,L_i)r_i
BV_(i+1)=f(∑_(i=1)^n P(K_i,L_i)r_i)

This shows that book value depends on expected income and external conditions (r_i, K_i, L_i).

4.3 Net Present Value (NPV)

NPV represents the sum of discounted net cash flows NCF_t for each period t: 

NPV=∑_(t=0)^N (NCF_t)/(1+r)^t

For continuous cash flows: 

NPV(r)=∫_(t=0)^∞ (1+r)^(-t) NCF(t)dt

Using the Laplace transform: 

F(s)={Lf}(s)=∫_0^∞ e^(-st) f(t)dt, where s=ln(1+i)

4.4 Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

IRR is the discount rate where NPV=0: 

∑_(t=0)^N (NCF_t)/(1+IRR)^t=0

For continuous cases: 

NPV(IRR)=∫_(t=0)^∞ (1+IRR)^(-t) NCF(t)dt

4.5 Investment Risk Estimation

For a random variable X, investment risk can be estimated as a function of its expected value E[X]. The most common measure is standard deviation σ, derived from variance V[X].

Discrete Distribution


σ=√(∑_(i=1)^n ((x_i−E[X])² p_i))
E(X)=∑_(i=1)^n x_i p_i
σ=√((∑_(i=1)^n x_i² p_i)−(E[X])²)

Continuous Distribution


σ=√(∫_(-∞)^(+∞)(x−E(x))² f(x)dx)
E(x)=∫_(-∞)^(+∞) x f(x)dx

In most investment projects, X represents profit levels, sales, or net income. The probability of outcomes depends on growth rates, exchange rates, and risks—political, macroeconomic, or technological.
